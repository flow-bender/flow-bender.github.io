FlowBender: Feedback-Aware Training
for Self-Correcting Conditional Flows

Daniel Gilo1 Sven Elflein2,3,4 Ido Sobol1 Or Litany1,2
1Technion 2NVIDIA 3University of Toronto 4Vector Institute
Paper Code BibTeX
TL;DR

We train conditional diffusion/flow models to read their own alignment error at inference time — a closed-loop correction policy that improves both condition fidelity and sample plausibility simultaneously, without the fidelity–plausibility trade-off of hand-tuned guidance.

Abstract

Conditional diffusion and flow models routinely fail to satisfy the very constraints that define their task. For instance, a depth-conditioned model often produces images whose re-extracted depth disagrees with the input, even though the forward operator — the depth predictor defining the constraint — is available during both training and inference. Existing approaches generally fall into two categories: supervised models that treat the conditioning signal as a static cue and ignore alignment information at inference, and guidance-based methods that consult it through hand-tuned linear updates, typically trading fidelity to the condition against the plausibility of the generated sample.

We argue that the fundamental gap in both paradigms is that the model is never trained to utilize its own alignment error. We introduce FlowBender, a closed-loop framework that treats this error as a first-class input, training the network to learn a correction policy conditioned on inference-time feedback. At each step, an unguided look-ahead pass estimates the clean signal, a task-specific deviation is computed via the forward operator, and a refinement pass consumes this signal to produce a corrected velocity.

We propose several variants, including a gradient-based formulation for differentiable operators and a zero-order variant for non-differentiable settings such as JPEG compression. For efficient sampling, we introduce a prior-step shortcut that enables closed-loop correction at minimal additional computational cost. Across image-to-image translation, restoration, and 3D mesh texturing, FlowBender consistently outperforms standard supervised baselines, alignment-loss-augmented training, and state-of-the-art inference-time guidance, improving fidelity and plausibility simultaneously rather than trading them against each other.

The Problem, Step by Step

A 2D toy walks through why existing paradigms fall short — and sets up what a closed-loop correction policy can achieve.

01

The Goal

The target is a multi-arm spiral. A measurement y = H(x) identifies which arm a sample belongs to. An ideal conditional model should cover each arc fully — generating diverse samples that lie precisely on the correct structure.

02

Standard Conditional FM Misses the Constraint

A model fine-tuned on supervised pairs learns to approximate the conditional distribution statistically, but is never asked to verify its output. Samples roughly follow the spiral shape but don't commit — each arm scatters around the true arc instead of lying precisely on it.

03

Inference-Time Guidance Has No Good Setting

Adding guidance steers trajectories toward the constraint using the operator's gradient — but the correction strength must be hand-tuned. At every scale something breaks: too weak and samples barely improve; stronger settings push samples into the wrong spiral arms entirely.

α = 0.5 — too weak

α = 2.0 — over-corrected

α = 4.0 — structure collapses

04

FlowBender: Both, Not Either

Trained to read its own alignment error as input, FlowBender learns a nonlinear correction policy — not a scalar guidance weight. Every arm lands tightly on its arc while covering the full extent of the posterior. Fidelity and plausibility, simultaneously, with nothing to tune.

Method

A two-pass training strategy that teaches the model to read and respond to its own alignment error at every denoising step.

Feedback-Aware Training

Two forward passes per denoising step. An unguided look-ahead estimates x̂1; the resulting alignment error st is fed back to a refinement pass that produces the corrected velocity. A stop-gradient on st and random nullification during training keep the unguided regime intact.

Feedback Variants

For differentiable operators, st is the gradient of the alignment loss — a first-order correction signal. For non-differentiable operators (e.g., JPEG compression), a zero-order variant uses the measurement residual R(H(x̂1), y) directly. A hybrid option concatenates both.

Inference Prior-Step Shortcut

Feedback signals are strongly correlated across adjacent timesteps. Below a threshold tthresh, the prior step's look-ahead estimate is reused rather than recomputed — reducing inference cost from 2N to N + 1 evaluations with minimal impact on quality.
